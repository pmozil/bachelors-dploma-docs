As shown in the tables \ref{tab:aes_throughput} and \ref{tab:aes_cycles},
the hardware acceleration achieves a consistent speedup of
approximately 5.25$\times$ across all buffer sizes from 128\,B to 16{,}768\,B,
reducing cycles per encrypted byte from roughly 2{,}670 to roughly 506.
The gain is directly attributable to the inner-loop transformation: in the
\texttt{-O3}-compiled software reference, each of the 16 state bytes processed by
SubBytes or MixColumns requires 3--4 GF$(2^8)$ arithmetic operations implemented as
a sequence of integer shifts, ANDs, and XORs --- approximately five instructions per byte.
The CXU instruction collapses this sequence to a single pipeline cycle per byte,
eliminating roughly 80 instructions per AES round

% P. M. Ну радше емпірично, я в дизасм дивився
% Цикл виконується 8 разів, а в ньому 10 інструкцій
The speedup is stable across input sizes, confirming that the fixed overhead of the
initial \texttt{cxidx} write is negligible relative to per-byte compute savings.

The AES configuration consumed a total of \textbf{20{,}948 logic cells} on the
Arty S7-50, with the \texttt{Cxu0} CXU module itself accounting for 209 cells ---
roughly 1\% of total device capacity.
The SoC-level LUT breakdown is: 423 LUT1, 801 LUT2, 2{,}052 LUT3, 1{,}962 LUT4,
1{,}583 LUT5, and 4{,}115 LUT6, plus 464 CARRY4 slices and 9{,}141 flip-flops
(FDCE/FDPE/FDRE/FDSE combined).
Block RAM usage stands at 13 RAMB18E1 and 23 RAMB36E1 tiles, and 4 DSP48E1
slices are occupied by the multiplier units.
Detailed per-module cell counts are provided in \autoref{tab:area_aes} in
\autoref{app:detailed_tables}.

This result is consistent with the broader literature on GF$(2^8)$-targeted custom
instructions.
Primas et al.~\cite{Primas2024NLUV} report a 68\% gain in the time-area product for
AES using the NLU-V extension on an RV32I core, which fuses the SubBytes and
MixColumns layers into a composite instruction; the CXU approach here targets the
finer-grained GF$(2^8)$ primitive, yielding a comparable cycle reduction with a
simpler hardware interface.
Marshall et al. report a 4$\times$ speedup for AES-128 on a comparable 32-bit
RISC-V core using scalar ISEs~\cite{Marshall2020AES}, rising to 10$\times$ on 64-bit
targets where state packing is more efficient; the CXU result of 5.25$\times$ on RV32
falls within this established range.
OS overhead in the Linux environment contributes only $\pm$2\% jitter with no
measurable change to the mean speedup factor.

The data in \autoref{fig:gzip_runtime} indicates that while the overall speedup is more modest than AES,
the CPU time for bit-stream manipulation decreased by approximately 10\%.
The smaller end-to-end gain reflects two structural factors: the LZ77 sliding-window
search --- a substantial fraction of total decompression work --- is not accelerated
by the current extension, and the end-to-end measurements include filesystem I/O,
which dilutes the compute-side improvement.
Profiling via \texttt{minstret} confirms that the four accelerated primitives account
for roughly 12--15\% of total decompression cycles in the inner loop, making the
observed 10\% end-to-end reduction consistent with near-complete elimination of
overhead within those primitives.

In terms of FPGA resource utilisation, the GZIP configuration occupied
\textbf{20{,}666 logic cells} in total, with the \texttt{Cxu0} module reduced to
only 20 cells --- a significantly smaller footprint than the AES CXU, reflecting the
simpler combinational nature of the four bit-manipulation primitives.
The LUT distribution was 447 LUT1, 689 LUT2, 1{,}989 LUT3, 1{,}935 LUT4, 1{,}732
LUT5, and 3{,}905 LUT6, with 417 CARRY4 slices and 9{,}143 flip-flops.
Block RAM and DSP usage is identical to the AES configuration (13 RAMB18E1,
23 RAMB36E1, 4 DSP48E1), as those resources are consumed by the fixed SoC
infrastructure rather than the CXU itself.
Full per-module instance areas are tabulated in \autoref{tab:area_gzip} in
\autoref{app:detailed_tables}.

\autoref{tab:tflite_results} confirms that, unless the CXU is also
connected to memory or other I/O modules, the gain is more evident
on compute-heavy tasks. Unlike the GZIP case, the CXU extension
covers a substantial part of the functionality of the benchmark,
yet it results only in a 13--15\% speedup.

This further proves that, unless a CXU module has access to the main RAM
or handles I/O in some other way, it would be limited by how compute-heavy
a task is.

The TFLite MAC configuration placed \textbf{20{,}698 logic cells} on the device.
Unlike the AES and GZIP experiments, this build contains no dedicated \texttt{Cxu0}
top-level instance; the MAC unit is folded directly into the VexiiRiscv pipeline
via the CXU plugin.
The LUT counts were 447 LUT1, 644 LUT2, 1{,}980 LUT3, 1{,}950 LUT4, 1{,}906
LUT5, and 3{,}835 LUT6, with 397 CARRY4 slices and 9{,}143 flip-flops.
The higher LUT5/LUT6 counts relative to the GZIP build reflect the multiplier
datapath within the fused MAC unit.
Complete instance-level cell counts are listed in \autoref{tab:area_tflite} in
\autoref{app:detailed_tables}.
